What’s this about?

This post is an extended version of my answer to a question about memory on Dyslexia Action’s Health Unlocked section:

Q: Does anyone have any tips for remembering information? I have to do an exercise qualification in work. To pass I have to learn the names and teaching points for 49 different exercises.

A more details answer is below. But if you’re busy, here’s the spoiler:

A: One well-known effective technique for remembering lots of information is forming rich associations. There are many ways technology can help with this. I recommend Memrise which is a free online service that also has great apps for Android, iPhone and iPad.

Memrise is easy to use, so you can just go to the site and use it. Or you can read on to find out more details.

Making Memories with Rich Associations

One of the recommended ways to remember many items is to form rich associations. This is also known as mnemonic devices. Everyone does this automatically but you can get much better at it if you practice.

Have you seen BBC’s Sherlock? He can remember masses of information because he places everything he wants to remember into a memory palace next to some other memorable thing. This is not fantasy. Memory palaces are an ancient device that people used to remember entire books before the days of the printing press when a book could be as expensive as a house.

Memory palaces are used even today by memory champions (yes, there are memory championships). American journalists Joshua Foer talk about how won the US Memory championship by putting the card he was memorizing in his mind next to Albert Einstein doing the moonwalk. He talks about that and more in this short video:

While using a full blown memory palace requires some training and a lot of commitment, you can get started with some of these tricks today, with an interesting service called Memrise.

Remembering with Memrise

Overview of Memrise

Memrise is an online tool that is designed to help you memorize lots of information. It is not that different from making flashcards but it makes them much more effective.

The key features of Memrise are:

Create your own decks of flashcards with words, images, sounds or even videos
Share your flashcard decks or used those created by others
Go through the flashcards and quiz yourself in many different ways
Add associations (called mems) to each flashcard (you can do this for your own or somebody else’s cards)
Load any flashcard decks onto your smartphone or tablet

Memrise quiz: The Heart of Memrise

But what makes Memrise special is its quiz function. It won’t let you try to memorise too many cards in row. As soon, as you’ve looked at 2 cards, it will start quizzing you in many different ways.

You can have a go with a Phonics card deck that is already on Memrise.

It will also give you periodic feedback.

But the stand out feature of Memrise is mems which is what they call associations. Here’s one example of a picture someone shared to remember the Chinese character for ‘measure’.

Each card can have multiple mems all created by different people. So you can benefit from the work of others.

Create your own

There are thousands of existing card decks on Memrise. People have created decks for all possible subjects from history, science, maths and every conceivable foreign language.

But It’s not very difficult to create your own cards. I created my own deck here in 30 minutes.

Alternatives to Memrise

There are also many useful alternatives to Memrise. Out of the hundreds flashcard apps, the most interesting is Anki which is free software that does not depend on a web service. Anki is available for all computer and phone platforms.

Don’t forget about paper flashcards, they are still as useful as ever. But Memrise can maybe give you some useful ideas about how to use them more effectively.

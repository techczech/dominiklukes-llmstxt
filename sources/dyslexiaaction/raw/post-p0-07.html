Tech Thursday: Audiobooks Pt 5: Apps for listening to audiobooks

Overview

This is the fifth and final post in a five-part series.

  1. Getting free audiobooks from local libraries via Overdrive
  2. Audiobook services for people with learning difficulties
  3. Audible review
  4. Alternatives to Audible
  5. Audiobook listening apps

Last four parts we looked at audiobooks from public libraries, specialised services for people with print disabilities, Audible, the biggest commercial provider and alternatives to Audible.

We are now going to look at 3 apps you can use to listen to audiobooks.

  • Listen Audiobook Player (Android, £1.20)
  • Bookmobile (iPad/iPhone, £3 in-app purchase)
  • Material Audiobook Player (Android, free)

Best audiobook app features

Playback features

You spend most of your time using the app to listen, so the most important features are those that let you listen the way you like to.

  • Resume a few seconds back after pause: Often your listening is interrupted by a call or a notification on your phone. When this happens in the middle of a word, it is best to resume a few seconds back. The smartest audiobook players vary this by the length of the interruption. Listen starts almost a minute back if you come back to a book a day later.
  • Skip back and forward buttons by 15-30 seconds: Unlike print, audiobooks are harder to scan. So it is important to be able to jump around quickly. If you miss something, you want to jump back a little and if you want to get to a place you know quickly, you need a jump forward. Personally, I found a jump back of 15 seconds to be best for catching up and jump forward of 30 seconds the best for quick browsing. Most apps offer some similar feature.
  • Setting speed of playback: I listen to all my audiobooks at 2x the speed and reduce it to 1.75x for faster readers or when I listen on the speakers in the car.  Some books I’ve listened to at almost 3x speed. I find it much easier to concentrate – and I get a lot more reading done. For those who are like me, a speed option is a must. For others, this is not an issue.
  • Control by headphone buttons: If you listen while walking with your headphones in, you want to be able to pause and jump back with the buttons on the headphones. This is much more important with books than music.

All the audiobook players reviewed in this article have the above features to a degree that most demanding audiobook listeners will find usable.

Playback configuration features

These are not essential features and you will not find them in every audiobook player but the best will have at least one or two.

  • Sleep timer: If you want to list to audiobooks in bed at night, you want to be able to set a timer so that you don’t lose track of where you are in the book
  • Shake to reset sleep timer: Sometimes you’re not yet asleep when the sleep timer runs out. So it’s great if you can just reach over and lift your phone briefly. The timer then resets and starts counting down again
  • Set bookmark on sleep: One audiobook player even sets a bookmark in the place you are in when you start the sleep timer. Otherwise it’s a good idea to set them manually
  • Set custom skip times: Not everyone has the same needs for the skip buttons. The best readers let you decide how far you can skip forward and back. Some only let you set the skip length the same for both directions
  • Volume boost: I often listen to lectures using the audiobook player and these are sometimes recorded too quietly. The same goes for some older audiobooks. Having an option to boost the volume is very useful.

Navigation features

Some people find these more important than others but all decent audiobook players have them.

  • Navigation by chapter: Most audiobooks mark the chapters in the file. If these are available, you should be able to navigate by them
  • Bookmarking: If you hear something interesting and want to come back to it, you should be able to set a bookmark. Bookmarks are also useful if you listen before you go to sleep.

Book management features

  • Manage audiobooks on device: All players have some sort of library feature. The best even let you delete or copy them
  • Import audiobooks from computer: Android audiobook players typically require you to use some other way for downloading the audiobook files to your device. On iOS they need to include some way of getting the files because it is not possible to get the files otherwise
  • Download audiobooks from store: Only the players that are attached to an audiobook store can also let you buy and download the audiobook files directly.

 

App feature comparison

This overview compares the features of the three apps we are looking at today with apps that come with the three top audiobook stores.

Listen Audiobook Player (Android, £1.20)

The Listen Audiobook Player is the most feature-rich audiobook player on any platform that I’ve come across.

It has every single feature a serious audiobook listener could want and lets you configure almost everything about it. Even the way it looks.

It even lets you sync audiobook positions across multiple devices using Google Drive.

This is the app that all others should look to for inspiration. One of its standout features is how it handles silence and speed settings.

The only downside is that you have to copy the audiobook files onto the device yourself. But you can add any folder on your device to the library.

Also, because there are so many settings, it may be hard to find the one you want quickly.

Alternatives

There are many audiobook player apps on Android. If you don’t like Listen, try one of these.

  • Smart Audiobook Player (this was my previous audiobook player, it is also very good but does not have quite such a nice interface)
  • Mort Audiobook Player (this was the first audiobook player app I used and now feels a bit dated).

kmobile (iPad/iPhone, £3)

Bookmobile is probably the best audiobook player for iPhones and iPads. It has all the key features and a very interesting interface. It is powerful and easy to use.

It does not have some of the really advanced features of Listen but it makes it very easy to get audiobooks on your device:

  • Download from your computer via a special link
  • Download a ZIP file from the web
  • Download free audiobooks using the Librivox catalog
  • Buy audiobooks from the iTunes library or from Listenup Audiobooks
  • Import files from iTunes library.

Another great unique feature is the setting of bookmarks on interruptions or when you start the sleep timer.

It even supports podcasts, although I would recommend a specialised podcast app for that.

Bookmobile is free to download and try but you have to pay £3 if you want to continue using it.

Alternatives

Some alternatives to Bookmobile are:

  • Bookmower (same as Bookmobile with podcast functionality removed)
  • Ambling BookPlayer Pro (also has Android app but not actively developed).

Material Audiobook Player (Android, free)

I decided to include Material Player to make sure that there is an option that is completely free.

I’ve used it to listen to a few books and found it just as competent for basic functionality as Listen. But it is not quite as configurable.

It has a very clean and easy to use design.

I would definitely recommend it as a starter app.

 

How to get audiobooks on your device

Android

None of the Android apps have a way of importing books from your device (Audible, Overdrive and Librivox connect to online libraries). You must point them at a folder on your phone where your audiobooks are stored.

One way to copy files to your phone is simply plug it in to your computer. You then drag and drop file into the phone folder.

The way I copy files is using an app called AirDroid. You open the app on your phone. Then you open a website web.airdroid.com, point the phone camera at the screen to connect them and then just drag and drop files into the web page.

You could also use Google Drive or Dropbox but the audiobook files are generally too large for that.

iPhone/iPad

There is no way to copy files directly to an iPhone or an iPad. The app you are using must support some sort of file import.

About

This is a comparison of two mobile apps: Claro ScanPen (iOS) and TextFairy (Android). These are then compared to the C-Pen Reader which is a dedicated hardware device.

Testing process

To test the two apps, I used the same texts I used for testing the C-Pen Reader. They were:

Printed out sample exam papers from AQA GCSE English
Printed out KS2 SPAG test
A random page out of Ian Smythe’s book Dyslexia in the Digital Age
Letter from a bank
Leaflet from the Water Company
2 business cards
Box of throat lozenges and the instructional leaflet.

Claro ScanPen (iOS)

Claro ScanPen is an iPhone and iPad only app designed specifically for people with dyslexia. It lets you take a picture of a page with text, touch the parts of text you want to read and it will read them out loud.

It does not do anything else. It won’t let you copy the text or highlight it. But it does what it does extremely well.

Key functions



Touch text to hear on a photo of a page
Take picture or import existing picture
Choose whether to select lines or individual words
Drag to select single line or multiple lines
Choose additional voices.

Secondary functions

The Claro ScanPen only does one thing. It does not have any additional functions.

Price

£5

Ease of Use

ScanPen is extremely easy to use. It is so simple, that there is no need to feature any screen shots in this article. All you see is on the screen is the picture of the text that you can touch.

Take a picture of a page with text (or import an existing picture)
Wait a few seconds for the app to figure the text out
Touch your finger to the lines or words and listen to them.

The only options in the settings are a choice between selecting whole lines or individual words. And you can buy additional voices.

The key issue, as with any scanning apps using the camera on the phone or tablet, is how steady can you hold the device while taking the picture? This makes it a bit easier to use on a phone but it is perfectly usable on a tablet, as well.

If you want to take a picture of a whole page without your hands shaking. It is easier to stand up.

Quality

ScanPen does a very good job at recognising text. The quality depends on both the quality of the picture and the quality of the text. But with most pages, it deals with very few errors. But they do creep up. Most often, I noticed, it spells out some words instead of speaking them.

Alternatives

There are no alternatives to the ScanPen’s key functionality (on any platform). But there are many apps that will scan and OCR a text on iPhones and iPads which fills a gap in ScanPen’s functionality. See a comparative review on MakeUseOf.com. See also this review of ScanBot.

Text Fairy (Android)

Text Fairy is a free and open source Android app that scans and recognises text. The text can then be read out in the app or saved and copied to another app. The built in reader is very good but does not highlight the text as it is being read. But the text is easy to export to more friendly readers.

Key functions



Take pictures of text and converts them to editable text
Reads out text
Store copied text
Share or copy the scanned text to other apps.

Secondary functions



Create a PDF from scanned text (this will have the picture of the text combined with the recognised text)
Change the text display settings
Add over 30 other languages (I tried Czech and the results were comparable with English).

Price

Free

Ease of Use

Text Fairy is very easy to use for the basic options. The process is very simple but involves more steps than any of the other options.

Take a picture of text (or import an existing picture)
Choose area to scan

With multi-column text, you also have to choose the order columns should be scanned in. This is very useful for business cards and newspapers.
Select whether text is 1 or 2 columns
Wait until text is scanned (5-10 seconds)
Choose an option of what to do with the text

Speak textShare text (to send to another app)Copy textCreate PDF.

The app will warn if the image is too fuzzy and gives you the option to take it again.

The picture of text does not have to be completely straight but it also does have to be steady. In this way, it is similar to ScanPen.

There are almost no settings, other than the option to download scanning add ins for other languages.

Quality

The quality of the output is amazing for full pages. There are almost no errors for flowing text and the recognition is very fast, but things are much worse with text with complex layout. It still does a great job with anything it recognises as text but does not always recognise what is text. It also occasionally confuses edges of images and borders with text and puts out gibberish. Still, it is perfectly usable even with more complex text.

Exam papers



Excellent for instructions and inserts. Barely usable with fill in the blank or multiple choice questions. The recognition is fine but getting the right text read out is a bit confusing.




Book page



Excellent. Quick, accurate and easy.




Letter from the bank



Excellent with some small imperfections around things like logos.




Leaflet



Fine. Struggled with complex layouts.




Business cards



Fine. Lot of work to select all the parts to scan. Sometimes did not recognise ends of lines as text.




Box of lozenges and instruction leaflet



Decent. Complex to take the parts but it did the best job of recognising text distorted by Braille dots.

Alternatives

There are many free and paid OCR apps on Android. I tried several and could not find any that matched the quality and flexibility of Text Fairy. However, you may like some additional features of apps like CamScanner. Some notetaking apps like Google Keep, OneNote and Evernote will also extract text from images.

Overall, the CPen Reader (see last week’s review) is the most versatile and the best in terms of quality and flexibility. It is almost flawless in quality and is easily the quickest from scan to speech. The only category where it is not as good or better than the others is whole page scanning (and of course price). It is not bad at whole page scanning but it takes the longest.

Text Fairy absolutely excels at whole page scanning and reading but is a bit cumbersome at most other tasks. Basically, if you want to read out a single line or a word, you have to scan the whole page. It ties with the CPen on scan quality overall and sometimes does a bit better. However, it is very cumbersome with very complex layouts such as printed objects and business cards. It takes the longest and the most steps to get from scan to speech.

Claro ScanPen is perhaps the easiest to use but falls down on quality. It is not at all bad or unacceptable but it makes the most errors by a small but noticeable margin. It also completely lacks the functionality to copy the text for later editing or making notes. It is quite quick at the speed of getting from scan to speech.

But, of course, for many people, the deciding factor will be price. Here, there is no contest. If you already have an Android device, Text Fairy is completely free. Just install it and use it. For those with an iPhone or iPad, there is no question. You should buy Scan Pen for £5. It is definitely worth the price. But should you spend £200 for a CPen Reader? As I said last week, this is definitely something schools should consider. As well as some individuals with specific needs. But for most people, the cost may be too high. University students should definitely look at the cheaper TS1 when it becomes available.

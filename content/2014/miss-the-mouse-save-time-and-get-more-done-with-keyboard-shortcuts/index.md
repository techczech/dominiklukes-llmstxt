How about saving hours and days of time by being more productive?
How about improving the health of your hands?
How about being less distracted?

All the experts agree, learning to use keyboard shortcuts can help you achieve all three. This post will teach you how.

See outline of this post in a mindmap.

Why use keyboard shortcuts

There are three main reasons why you should use keyboard shortcuts, instead of the mouse.

Get things done faster
Focus better on your work
Save your wrists

The mouse is great but it takes time. You may think it’s nothing but all those seconds of moving your hand from the keyboard to the mouse add up.

Also surprisingly, using the mouse strains muscles and tendons you maybe didn’t even know you had. I’ve had the pain of Repetitive Stress Injury – and believe me, it’s no fun at all!

What most people don’t realize is that you can get everything done with just the keyboard. Some things are best suited for a mouse but when you want to write a document or fill in a form – the mouse just gets in the way.

Things the mouse is for

Clicking on links on webpages
Moving images on the screen
Choosing from complicated menu screens

Things the keyboard shortcuts are for

Moving the cursor around while writing (Ctrl, ArrowKeys, Home, End, etc.)
Filling in forms (Tab to jump forward, Shift-Tab to jump back, Enter to submit)
Selecting, copying and moving the text while writing (Ctrl-C, Ctrl-X, Ctrl-V, etc.)
Formatting the text while writing (Ctrl-B, etc.)
Adjusting windows on screen (Win- Arrow Keys)
Moving and renaming files (Arrow Keys, F2, Ctrl-C,Ctrl-V)
Switching from one application to another (Alt-Tab)
Opening menus and choosing options (Alt + underlined letters in the menu titles)

How to type shortcuts

One of the reasons many people give up on typing shortcuts is because they don’t do it right. These are the right steps:

Hold control keys first
Then type the shortcut key
DON'T type all keys at once

Most important shortcuts

Universal

These will work in any application on any computer (on Macs, substitute the Apple key for Ctrl). Remember Ctrl-Z works in all sorts of unexpected places and can save you a lot of trouble by undoing things you didn’t mean to do.

Text Manipulation

Once you start using shortcuts while typing, you will be horrified how ineffective you’ve been up to now. It will take a bit of time to become automatic at this, but once you do, you will never go back.

One of the advantages of keyboard shortcuts while editing text is that you can be much more precise. Have you ever tried to select just a part of a word with the mouse? It can take several tries. But if you just hold down Shift while hitting the left/right arrow, you can select even a single letter with ease. And instead of hitting Delete twenty times to delete a line simply hit Home while holding down Shift and you just have to hit Delete once.

My favourite in Word is Alt-Shift-Up/Down. Try it. It moves the whole paragraph the cursor is in up or down. You don’t even have to select anything!

Windows

These shortcuts work anywhere in Windows. Learn to love the Window key. Do you want to compare two texts side by side? You can arrange the windows with a mouse but it will take you a while. By using Win-Left/Right, you can make a Window fit perfectly.

Or try Win-Up. It will make the window fit the full monitor.

If you’re using two monitors (and you should be), adding Shift to the Win key means you can easily switch open windows from one monitor to the other or fit just the height of the monitor! It’s like magic.

(Note: You have to have Windows 7 for some of these.)

How to learn keyboard shortcuts easily and without stress

Many people see the long list of shortcuts and they are horrified but, as I said, it’s shocking how easy it is to learn them. They will soon become a part of your muscle memory. I find it hard to remember even a short list but over the years, I’ve learned several hundred shortcuts.

Here are some strategies you can use to start your journey of learning shortcuts.

Shortcut = first letter and other mnemonics

Most shortcuts begin with the first letter of the command. For example, Ctrl-C for Copy or Ctrl-B for Bold. Sometimes, you can use other tricks. Ctrl-X for Cut because X looks like scissors and Ctrl-V for paste because V looks like a tube of paste. Sometimes shortcuts will use another letter in the command like Ctrl-E for Center or Ctrl-K to insert a Link in Word.

Force yourself to type - Unplug the mouseTry unplugging the mouse for a short period to encourage yourself to use the keyboard instead.

Look under menus and in settings

Commands in menus will often have the shortcuts next to them. Sometimes, there are shortcuts listed in the settings. Sometimes you can even change them to suit your needs.

Google ""Shortcuts for...""

Wondering what the best shortcuts are for the software you use? Just Google it. You will find that somebody’s shared their favourites or the company has a list.

Productivity and Accessibility Cue Cards

Download and print out the Productivity and Accessibility Cue Cards. They have the most important shortcuts in different categories and other tips.

Memrise

I took the most important shortcuts and created a card deck on Memrise. You can use it to help you memorise the most important ones in a fun interface. It even works as an app on your smartphone and tablet.
